Geometric vs Linear Cumulative Mass Function

Comparing a fixed-probability (geometric) system to a scaling linear cumulative mass function (LCMF), where the success chance grows with each consecutive failure. LCMF uses P = b / (1 − b·n), with n = failures since last success. On all charts, the x-axis is the attempt number since the last success (n = 1 is the first attempt after a success). For LCMF, the per-attempt chance is clamped to [0, 1] — once the denominator hits 0 or goes negative, success is guaranteed.

Expected number of successes after n attempts

At low n, the LCMF's expected number of successes is slightly below the geometric line. This is the cost of cutting off the geometric distribution's long tail: to keep the mean inter-success time matched, the up-front success chance has to be lower than the geometric's per-attempt chance. The early attempts are "paying" for the guarantee that no run will go absurdly long.

Scale up the x-axis max and the gap stops growing — their asymptotic slope is identical (both = 1 / mean attempts to success). The LCMF line stays slightly below the geometric throughout, but it tracks it in parallel: in the long run the two systems produce the same rate of successes, just offset by the early-n deficit.
